# Liminal-horror spatial and systems review

This review separates published evidence and developer postmortems from project
design inference. Research does not prescribe a single frightening floor plan;
it gives constraints that make the procedural choices more legible, navigable,
and capable of sustaining tension.

## What the evidence supports

- **Boundaries and landmarks reduce disorientation.** A VR navigation study
  found the greatest pointing error in an open field and better orientation in
  rooms with walls, landmarks, or even a floor-texture boundary. Project
  inference: large volumes should expose a perimeter, structural bay, material
  edge, or visible route rather than become cue-less terrain. Source: [Boundaries Reduce
  Disorientation in Virtual Reality](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/virtual-reality/articles/10.3389/frvir.2022.882526/full).
- **Scale works as a transition, not a constant.** Experiments with changing
  enclosure and ceiling height found that spatial changes alter arousal and
  valence. Project inference: use compression-release sequences so ordinary
  rooms make one exceptional high or open space meaningful. Source: [Measuring arousal and
  valence generated by dynamic experience of architectural
  forms](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9352685/).
- **Pillars control prospect, refuge, and occlusion.** Environmental-perception
  studies distinguish visual from locomotive permeability and associate lower
  prospect/more hiding opportunities with perceived danger. A column field is
  useful, by project inference, when it creates uncertain occlusion around a
  readable route; endless columns are not inherently scary. Sources: [Danger, fear and preference in a
  simulated environment](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0272494410000460)
  and [Visual Permeability, Locomotive Permeability, Safety, and
  Enclosure](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0013916505276741).
- **Perceptible exposure makes vertical depth effective.** Height-simulation
  studies report stronger anxiety from increasing height, downward views,
  exposed platforms, and mesh footing. Project inference: preserve visible
  depth and exposed edges; shadow and vertical audio are additional design
  hypotheses, not findings from these sources.
  Sources: [Height Simulation in a VR
  CAVE](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/human-neuroscience/articles/10.3389/fnhum.2018.00372/full)
  and [The Virtual Pole](https://www.jvrb.org/jvrb/article/view/14.2017.6).
- **Visible circulation improves multilevel wayfinding.** Strategic visibility
  is most helpful when it reveals actionable routes such as stairs. Project
  rule: a player may see an unreachable destination across a void, but an
  entrance should reveal or strongly cue how vertical travel works. Source: [Strategic visibility in
  multilevel buildings](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-53420-6).
- **Landmarks work best at decisions.** Distinctive, asymmetric landmarks on
  important routes and junctions reduce navigation time and errors. Sources:
  [Influence of Landmarks on
  Wayfinding](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2017.01220/full)
  and [Vinson's landmark-design guidelines](https://arxiv.org/pdf/cs/0304001).
- **Topology should precede geometry.** Graph-grammar and constraint-based
  procedural systems plan intended connections, prescribed rooms, stairs, and
  rare multi-cell features before generic fill. Sources: [Adventures in Level
  Design](https://pcgworkshop.com/archive/dormans2010adventures.pdf), [Procedural
  Level Design in
  Eldritch](https://media.gdcvault.com/gdc2015/presentations/Pittman_David_Procedural%20Level%20Design.pdf),
  and [Infinigen
  Indoors](https://openaccess.thecvf.com/content/CVPR2024/papers/Raistrick_Infinigen_Indoors_Photorealistic_Indoor_Scenes_using_Procedural_Generation_CVPR_2024_paper.pdf).
- **Continuous threat tends to flatten pacing.** Horror developers use peaks,
  fades, recovery, fake-outs, and constrained recipe selection instead of
  firing events continuously. Project inference: apply the same contrast
  principle to open-space reveals; these sources do not establish an emptiness
  threshold. Sources: Frictional's [Four
  Ways to Design for Horror](https://www.gamedeveloper.com/design/four-ways-to-design-for-horror-from-i-amnesia-i-dev-frictional-games),
  Valve's [Left 4 Dead AI Director
  presentation](https://cdn.steamstatic.com/apps/valve/2009/GDC2009_ReplayableCooperativeGameDesign_Left4Dead.pdf),
  and Motive's [Dead Space Intensity
  Director](https://www.ea.com/ea-studios/motive/news/inside-dead-space-4-the-intensity-director).
- **Corpus analysis exposes global bias.** Small local generation rules can
  unexpectedly dominate the output distribution, so representative seeds and
  worst cases matter more than a few hand-picked maps. Source: [Analyzing the
  Expressive Range of a Level
  Generator](https://www.pcgworkshop.com/archive/smith2010analyzing.pdf).

## Diagnosis of the previous generator

In a pre-v14 exploratory project audit, world-gen v13 had a strong room planner
and robust multilevel contracts, but its zone layer could replace the room
network with a separate open-world fabric.
Across sampled 240x240-chunk maps, connected non-office runs reached 86-165
chunks and fully open regions extended for kilometres. A 128-seed 9x9 spawn
sample included 12 seeds with no office chunk at all; one representative seed
was 81/81 warehouse and 91.7% `CELL_OPEN`.

The seam audit still rated that case near perfect because its job was to prove
continuity. This is the key distinction:

```text
topological correctness: can the player move through it?
expressive correctness: is the space bounded, paced, legible, and varied?
```

Opening walls or removing columns can repair connectivity, but cannot repair
experiential over-openness.

## Implemented direction: room fabric plus bounded macro archetypes

World-gen v14 changes the macro contract:

1. Office districts and their semantic rooms are the continuous background
   architecture.
2. A 6x6-chunk macro district may reserve an ordinary 1-2-by-1-2-chunk pillar
   hall. A 30% hero roll expands the footprint to 3-4 chunks on each axis; only
   hero footprints may become warehouse courts.
3. Only one checkerboard parity of districts is eligible on a floor, so no
   elected pockets are cardinal neighbours.
4. Every pocket retains an office margin. Under the shipped profile, ordinary
   footprints contain at most four non-office chunks and hero footprints at
   most sixteen.
5. The 3x3 chunks around the spawn column are always office, and a 9x9 spawn
   corpus invariant requires a clear room majority.
6. Warehouse courts receive a pillar perimeter, so the cue-less inner volume is
   smaller than its visible architectural boundary and never meets offices
   directly.
7. Pillar halls use wide structural bays and typed monumental piers. The large
   piers have their real 2.2-unit width in rendering, swept collision, placement,
   line of sight, path smoothing, the explored map, and the debug map.
   Landmark-level signatures choose a coherent monumental grid, two-row
   processional axis, broken center bay, or court colonnade rather than mixing
   every pier independently.
8. The shared patch report includes a separate `architecture` section with
   office/open share, longest open run, largest open component, and an `ok`
   verdict. Seam correctness remains independently scored, so a high continuity
   score is no longer mistaken for evidence of acceptable spatial density.
9. Bridge-less 4-15-storey shafts culminate in two low guard rails on the top
   axial gallery. They create a deliberate downward overlook while leaving slab
   masks, circulation, collision safety, and full-height streaming unchanged.

The v14 landmark descriptor currently reserves a zone-level footprint and
records its kind, intensity role, axis, and pier signature. It does not yet
author entrance alignment, circulation, or graph adjacency inside the office
semantic plan; that integration remains roadmap work. In particular, a
processional axis is visually framed but is not yet promised to line up with an
office threshold.

These limits are project tuning decisions, not findings from the cited papers.
The shipped audit treats 75% office share as a catastrophic floor on sufficiently
large patches, not as an expected mean. `npm run audit:world` measures the
operating distribution and retains worst seed IDs; hard shipped-profile
invariants remain 2x2 ordinary footprints, 4x4 hero footprints, and no
cardinally adjacent elected districts.

## Structure roadmap

Future structures should become reserved semantic nodes, not late decorative
stamps. The following are implementation-ready contracts, not claims about what
the current macro archetypes already author:

| Structure | Spatial contract | Horror function | Required cue |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Hypostyle hall | Bounded perimeter, wide column bays, axial route, one missing/offset bay | Occlusion and anticipation | Distinct damaged or unlit bay at a decision |
| Split-level overlook | Sunken lower room, U-gallery, visible stair, destination across void | Exposure plus deferred access | Stair or route visible/cued from entrance |
| Service mezzanine | Partial catwalk, machinery, one crossover | Vertical sound and silhouette staging | Audible/visible activity above or below |
| Compression-release suite | 2-4 ordinary/low rooms into one high hall, constrained side exit | Makes scale change into a beat | Threshold frames the reveal and exit |
| Twin-void atrium | Two offset shafts separated by an occupied spine | Layered observation and route choice | Spine and next landing remain legible |
| Repetition-anomaly wing | Repeat one room grammar, then alter one major property | Recognition, doubt, and memory | Change is structural/light/acoustic, not a tiny prop |
| Service pocket | Utility/storage branch off the main route | Optional suspense without deleting room density | Strongly distinct sound/material role |

The bounded hypostyle footprint, coherent pier patterns, and broken-bay variant
are implemented. Entrance alignment, a deliberately unlit/damaged decision bay,
and the other structure families remain roadmap work.

Each future descriptor should retain at least:

```text
kind, semanticRole, footprint, floorSpan, entrances,
visibleCirculationRequirement, occlusionProfile,
landmarkSignature, intensityRole, minimumGraphSpacing
```

Structures that cut slabs must remain canonical root-seeded contracts, reserve
stairs and circulation before room allocation, own their global seam geometry,
and expose exact masks to streaming, grounding, lighting, sight, navigation,
minimaps, and layered audits.

## Dynamics and feature roadmap

### 1. Intensity director

The first small slice is implemented: a level-seeded ambient cue director
suppresses distant fake-outs during visible/high-tension threat and real
cross-floor footfalls, requires six uninterrupted recovery seconds, spaces its
opportunities, and abstains 30% of the time. It does not alter enemy behavior or
claim to be the complete director below.

Track experienced intensity separately from enemy proximity. Inputs should
include recent chase/sighting, sanity loss, sprinting, flashlight exposure,
hesitation, backtracking, vertical separation, and time since a quiet room. Use
`build -> peak -> fade -> relax` states. At high intensity, suppress new active
threats and bias toward a navigable recovery room; after prolonged calm, emit a
cue before choosing whether it pays off.

Geometry stays deterministic. The director selects from sockets authored by the
space descriptor:

- distant footstep on another floor;
- light failure without an enemy;
- silhouette across an atrium or behind a pillar;
- sound behind the player with no payoff;
- short pursuit that terminates at a threshold;
- deliberately empty recovery beat.

Include abstention, vary cue-to-payoff delay, and prohibit identical high-impact
recipes back-to-back.

### 2. Semantic room roles

Extend generic office rooms into service, storage, meeting, transition,
recovery, junction-landmark, and anomaly-candidate roles. Roles should affect
adjacency, doors, lighting rhythm, acoustics, event sockets, and clutter, while
collision continues to consume the same thin-wall raster.

### 3. Repetition with controlled mutation

Select a room motif at district scope and repeat it two to four times. Mutate one
high-salience property using a separate stable random stream: ceiling/slab,
door count, column bay, lamp color/failure, material band, or acoustic response.
Critical progress must never depend on noticing a subtle mutation.

### 4. Vertical acoustic cues

Tall atria already stream their full height. Attach positional hums, impacts,
footsteps, and reverb profiles to canonical floors/void lobes so an unseen level
becomes perceptible before an enemy appears. Sound sources need the same
structure-aware floor gating as sight and lamps.

## Validation gates

Run thousands of seeds and retain median, 95th percentile, maximum, and the
worst seed IDs for:

- room/corridor/lobby versus special-space share;
- largest connected open component and longest axial open run;
- sightline-length distribution and visible-neighbourhood size;
- graph cycles, dead ends, articulation points, and decision density;
- distance and room-node count between hero structures;
- consecutive structure-kind repetition;
- landmark visibility on junction approaches;
- vertical-circulation visibility near multilevel entrances;
- exit distance and visibility from the deepest route point;
- generator time, plan-cache hit rate, resident tall-structure chunks, and draw
  calls.

Use hard caps for catastrophic cases and inspect the worst one percent visually.
A blended quality score is useful for tuning but must not allow excellent seam
continuity to compensate for an unbounded empty field.
